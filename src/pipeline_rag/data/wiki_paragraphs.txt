Frank Herbert (October 8, 1920 – February 11, 1986) was an American science-fiction author best known for creating the Dune universe. His debut novel, Dune (1965), won the Hugo Award and has sold over 12 million copies worldwide. Herbert went on to write five sequels, exploring themes of politics, religion and ecology in a far-future setting.

Dune is a 1965 science-fiction novel by Frank Herbert. Set on the desert planet Arrakis, it follows Paul Atreides as he navigates political intrigue, mystical visions and control of the spice mélange. The book is celebrated for its complex world-building, environmental allegory and influence on subsequent sci-fi media.

J. K. Rowling (born July 31, 1965) is a British author best known for writing the Harry Potter series. Her first book, Harry Potter and the Philosopher’s Stone (1997), introduced readers to the young wizard and his adventures at Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry. The seven-book saga has sold over 500 million copies and inspired films, theme parks and a global fan community.

Harry Potter and the Philosopher’s Stone (1997) is the first novel in J. K. Rowling’s Harry Potter series. It tells of an orphaned boy who learns on his eleventh birthday that he is a wizard and begins magical schooling. Themes include friendship, courage and the fight against dark forces in a richly imagined version of modern Britain.

Paulo Coelho (born August 24, 1947) is a Brazilian lyricist and novelist whose works have been translated into 80 languages. He achieved global fame with his novel The Alchemist (1988), a philosophical tale about a shepherd’s quest for treasure and personal destiny. Coelho’s writing blends spirituality, mysticism and simple parables, making him one of the world’s best-selling authors.

The Alchemist is a 1988 novel by Paulo Coelho that follows Santiago, a shepherd boy, on a journey from Spain to the Egyptian desert. After recurring dreams about hidden treasure, he meets mentors who teach him to listen to his heart and follow omens. The novel has sold over 65 million copies and remains a celebrated work of magical realism.

Pedro Álvares Cabral (c. 1467–1520) was a Portuguese navigator and explorer remembered as the first European to reach Brazil in 1500. Commanding a fleet bound for India, he landed on the Brazilian coast and claimed it for the Portuguese crown. His voyage opened the way for Portugal’s colonization of South America.

The Amazon Rainforest spans nine South American countries and covers over 5.5 million square kilometers. Known as the “lungs of the Earth,” it produces 20% of the planet’s oxygen and hosts unparalleled biodiversity. Indigenous tribes, rivers like the Amazon, and unique ecosystems make it a focus of environmental conservation.

Christopher Columbus (1451–1506) was an Italian explorer sponsored by Spain who completed four voyages across the Atlantic between 1492 and 1504. In 1492 he landed in the Bahamas, mistakenly believing he had reached Asia, and later explored parts of the Caribbean. His expeditions initiated sustained European contact with the Americas.

The Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–1806), led by Meriwether Lewis and William Clark, was the first American overland journey to the Pacific coast. Commissioned by President Thomas Jefferson after the Louisiana Purchase, the Corps of Discovery mapped rivers, cataloged wildlife, and established relations with Indigenous nations. Their detailed journals advanced U.S. westward expansion.

Marco Polo (c. 1254–1324) was a Venetian merchant and explorer whose travels to the court of Kublai Khan were recorded in “The Travels of Marco Polo.” Between 1271 and 1295 he journeyed across Central Asia and China along the Silk Road, introducing Europe to Mongol culture, porcelain, spices and coal. His accounts fueled European interest in Asian trade routes.

Zheng He (1371–1433) was a Chinese admiral of the Ming dynasty who led seven grand voyages between 1405 and 1433. Commanding a fleet of treasure ships, he visited Southeast Asia, South Asia, the Middle East and East Africa, establishing maritime trade and tributary relations. His expeditions demonstrated China’s naval power and facilitated cultural exchange.

The Silk Road was an ancient network of trade routes connecting China, Central Asia, the Middle East and Europe from the 2nd century BCE to the 14th century CE. Caravans carried silk, spices, precious metals and ideas, fostering economic and cultural interactions. Named for China’s prized silk exports, these routes shaped the course of world history.

Ferdinand Magellan (c. 1480–1521) was a Portuguese explorer who organized the first circumnavigation of the Earth under the Spanish flag. Beginning in 1519 with five ships, his expedition sailed through the Strait of Magellan into the Pacific and reached the Philippines. Magellan was killed there in 1521, but his crew completed the voyage, proving global connectivity by sea.

Amerigo Vespucci (1454–1512) was an Italian navigator and cartographer whose name was given to the continents of North and South America. On voyages between 1499 and 1502, he recognized that the lands discovered by Columbus constituted a “New World.” His letters describing coastal explorations helped revise European maps of the era.
